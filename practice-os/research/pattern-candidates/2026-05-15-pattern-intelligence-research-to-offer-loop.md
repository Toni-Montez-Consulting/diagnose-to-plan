# Pattern Intelligence: Research-To-Offer Loop

Use this when an external company, product, tool, market move, or workflow
shift seems useful but should not become public copy, a product bet, or an
implementation project until it has been translated into practical consulting
judgment.

## Observation

- What was observed: Glean clarified that useful AI companies can prove broad
  work patterns without being copied directly.
- Where it showed up: Toni's follow-on note about building a research arm for
  the consulting practice.
- Why it caught attention: the practice needs a way to learn from AI/software
  companies without chasing tools, copying startups, or building premature
  SaaS.
- Who or what type of operator it may apply to: founder-led, builder-led, or
  small-team consulting practices that need market intelligence to become
  practical offers, diagnostics, workflows, and handoff assets.

## Underlying Principle

- What seems to be true: the most useful research is not passive consumption;
  it is translation into an artifact that improves practice judgment.
- What business or human behavior is underneath it: operators do not need more
  information as much as they need better decisions, better language, better
  workflows, and sharper questions.
- What would make the principle false: if the research does not change a
  diagnostic, workflow, offer, template, runbook, client explanation, or
  backlog decision, it is probably just interesting noise.

## Consulting Translation

- How this changes discovery: ask which outside product pattern is relevant
  only after naming the client's real workflow pain.
- How this changes diagnosis: look for the workflow that was collapsed, not the
  feature that looks impressive.
- How this changes delivery or handoff: build the small-business version that a
  client can operate, not the enterprise product shape.
- How this changes messaging or client education: explain market movement in
  plain language: what got easier, what still needs judgment, and what the
  owner should do next.
- What Toni should watch for next time: companies that turn scattered work into
  one useful operating surface, reduce repeated human bottlenecks, or convert
  messy input into decision-ready artifacts.
