## Required Email Summary Pattern

When a real client/prospect email comes in, summarize it before drafting:

1. What they said
2. What they want
3. What changed
4. Recommended response
5. Next artifact

This pilot does not replace that rule. It only gives better language once the
summary is clear.

## Drafting Principle

Use this framing:

> AI is useful when it is attached to real work someone owns.

Say the mechanism plainly:

- map the workflow;
- identify where judgment, handoff, or review breaks down;
- decide where AI can reduce drag;
- keep human review in the loop;
- deliver something the business can actually operate.

Avoid this framing:

- "AI transformation"
- "AI-powered growth"
- "we unlock efficiency"
- "agentic future of work"
- "AI can do everything for you"

## Reusable Prospect Reply Block

Use this when a prospect is curious about AI but has not yet clarified the
workflow.

```text
The way I usually approach AI is pretty practical: I do not start by asking
which AI tool you want. I start by looking at the work that is currently slow,
manual, unclear, or stuck in someone's head.

From there, we can decide whether AI actually belongs in the workflow. If it
does, the goal is to use it in a way that supports the business: clearer
intake, faster drafting or review, better routing, cleaner handoffs, or a
system your team can operate without guessing.

The important part is that human judgment stays built into the process. AI can
make the work smoother, but it should not turn the business into a black box.
```

## Executive-Friendly Version

Use this when the recipient needs the point quickly.

```text
My recommendation is to treat AI as part of the operating system, not as a
standalone feature.

Before building, I would map the workflow, identify the decisions or handoffs
that create drag, and then decide where AI can help without removing human
review. That keeps the work explainable and gives us a clearer path to measure
whether the system is actually useful.
```

## Client Education Note

Use this as a mini-explainer in a Blueprint, action packet, or follow-up note.

```text
AI is most useful when it is connected to a real workflow.

For this kind of work, the question is not "can we add AI?" The better question
is: where is the business losing time, clarity, or trust because the current
process depends too much on memory, manual handoffs, or repeated judgment?

Once that is clear, AI can support the system in specific ways: summarizing,
drafting, routing, checking, organizing, or helping someone make a better next
decision. The system still needs clear ownership, human review, and a handoff
the business can understand.
```

## Short Highlight Version

```text
AI should make the workflow clearer and easier to operate. It should not make
the business harder to understand.
```

## Questions This Language Should Trigger

- What work is currently stuck in the owner's head?
- Where does the workflow slow down because someone has to remember, interpret,
  or manually route the next step?
- What should AI help with: intake, drafting, review, routing, analysis,
  follow-up, or decision support?
- Who reviews the AI-assisted output before it becomes real?
- What would make this system easier for another person to operate?
