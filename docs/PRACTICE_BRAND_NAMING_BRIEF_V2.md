## Why The Prior Name Pass Did Not Land

Names like `Veyra`, `Praxis`, `Noema`, `Nexora`, `Meridian`, `Argos`, and
`Parallax` moved in the right direction emotionally, but the user reaction was
correct: they were still too abstract or too already-seen.

The issue was not that they sounded bad. The issue was that they did not create
enough immediate meaning for the business:

- "Cool name, but what does it mean?"
- "I feel like I have seen this company name before."
- "I cannot passionately explain this yet."

## Research Takeaways

The best tech names are not necessarily literal, but they usually attach to a
clear belief or category move.

- `OpenAI` is close to literal: category plus belief. It is immediately about
  AI, and its public about page anchors the company around AI research and
  deployment.
- `Anthropic` is more abstract, but it points toward human-centered AI safety
  and reliable systems.
- `Meta` is broad, but the rebrand was attached to a strategic horizon: the
  metaverse and a company-level shift beyond one app.
- USPTO guidance says stronger marks are usually suggestive, fanciful, or
  arbitrary, while descriptive names are weaker and harder to protect.

Naming lesson:

> Do not choose a random beautiful word. Choose an ownable word that can absorb
> the practice's belief.

For this practice, the belief is:

> Work trapped in someone's head should become a system people can actually
> run.

### Source Board Takeaways

The strongest path is probably not a direct compound like `Runmade`, even
though `Runmade` captures the ethos. The better shape is:

- original company name first;
- thesis/tagline explains the work;
- name feels sharp, intelligent, and human;
- the name can become ownable through repeated use;
- no generic service suffix;
- no direct `work/run/form/path/system` mashup unless it feels unusually
  inevitable.

Useful name types for Round 3:

1. **Principle word**: like `Linear`, but warmer and less project-tool coded.
2. **Human intelligence word**: like `Notion` or `Anthropic`, but more practical
   and less academic.
3. **Operational metaphor**: like `Palantir`, but less dark and more useful.
4. **Simple object word**: like `Stripe`, if the object can quietly imply
   alignment, structure, or transfer.
5. **Coined company word**: like `Vercel`, but with less startup-noise and a
   stronger human/operational undertone.

Sound profile to test:

- 2-3 syllables;
- easy to say once heard;
- crisp consonant or memorable vowel shape;
- no awkward compound seam;
- not too many `x`, `z`, or forced sci-fi letters;
- can sit naturally before "helps".

Example sentence:

> [Name] helps turn the work in your head into systems your business can
> actually run.

## Naming Strategy

Updated target style:

- tech-company original;
- sharp/intelligent with a human element;
- explainable by the tagline rather than fully descriptive in the name;
- ownable enough to become meaningful through use;
- not a generic service label;
- not an AI-only label;
- calm, expert, useful, and premium;
- company-feeling without pretending to be a giant firm;
- able to hold future software, AI workflow, consulting, and operating-system
  offers.

Avoid:

- personal-name-led primary brands;
- `Solutions`, `Consulting`, `Agency`, or `AI` as the primary name;
- pure abstract syllables that need too much explanation;
- direct word-mashups that feel generated from the thesis;
- crowded roots like `Nex`, `Praxis`, `Meridian`, `Wayline`, `WorkMade`,
  `FlowWright`, or `PathCraft`;
- names that sound like a SaaS task app instead of an expert practice.

### Round 3C Hard Read

The deeper pattern is now clearer:

- Real intellectual/classical words carry meaning and founder conviction, but
  they become crowded fast and can feel academic.
- Lightly coined root-based names are the highest-risk lane: they often become
  either fake-sounding or one spelling step away from an existing AI/consulting
  company.
- Modern English seriousness is emotionally clearer, but it can become too
  generic, descriptive, or already owned by larger brands.

The likely winner is not simply "ancient" or simply "coined." It is probably a
word with intellectual provenance, modern restraint, and a founder explanation
that feels inevitable.

The target sentence is:

> I started [Name] because the work in people's heads should become clear,
> usable systems their business can actually run.

The name needs to make that sentence feel more confident, not more clever.

## Round 3K: Sound-First / Real-Word Reset

Status: next naming method, not candidate approval.

Definition:

> Sound-first means the first test is not "what does this word mean?" The first
> test is "does this sound like a credible, sharp, memorable company Toni would
> feel good saying out loud?"

Round 3K should generate names before explaining them. Meaning can be backfilled
only after the name creates emotional pull.

Rules:

1. Allow both simple real English words and invented names.
2. Do not start from Greek, Latin, Sanskrit, Japanese, or Chinese root mining.
3. Do not include `AI`, `systems`, `solutions`, `labs`, `consulting`, or
   `studio` in the core name.
4. Avoid names that sound motivational, sentimental, wellness-coded,
   mythological for its own sake, or like a fake SaaS generator.
5. Prefer two- or three-syllable names that feel serious, modern, human, and
   easy to say.
6. Test the name out loud in phrases like:
   - "[Name] helps turn capability into working systems."
   - "I run [Name]."
   - "Book a diagnostic with [Name]."
   - "[Name] built the workflow we use to run this."

Allowed directions:

- simple English words that can be owned by execution;
- short invented words that feel natural rather than mashed together;
- private-code names inspired by phrases like "capability made real,"
  "relentless care," "build what runs," and "persist without exception";
- category-creation names that sound like a new way of working, not a service
  description.

Round 3K prompt:

> Generate names that sound like a serious modern company before they explain
> themselves. Include simple English words and invented names. Only keep names
> Toni can imagine saying with confidence.
